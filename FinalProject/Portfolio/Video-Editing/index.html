The Race to Beat Silent Clubstep (Geometry Dash)

This documentary video dives into the 8-year history of "Silent Clubstep," an infamous Geometry Dash level that many players had believed to be humanly impossible since its conception. The video breaks down the timeline of how players slowly chipped away at the level, tracking the world records, the evolution of top-level playing meta, and the eventual verification that changed the game’s history. The goal was to take years of scattered events and data and turn it into a high-stakes, cinematic story that anyone could follow, even if they’ve never played the game.

This was my first major project in long-form storytelling and a huge turning point for my YouTube channel. I handled the entire production solo — doing all the research, writing the script, conducting interviews, and editing the entire video. It was a massive project, but it taught me how to manage a heavy workflow and keep a nearly 40-minute video engaging from start to finish. I made this video in 2022 during my 11th grade year, and it is currently my most-viewed video of all time — and one of the videos I'm most proud of.

Guess the Difficulty of This Level, Win $100!

In this challenge-style video, I gathered 10 professional Geometry Dash players and had them guess the difficulty of 6 different levels they'd never seen before. For reference, there are 14 different difficulty ratings in the game, and for every rating a player was off by, 1 point would be added to their score (points in this game are BAD). In the end, whoever achieved closest to a perfect score would receive $100.

This was the first time I had ever made a video like this. Managing a project of this scale was a step up in my workflow. I coordinated with 10 creators to capture their live reactions and commentary, meaning I had to balance multiple audio tracks and differing personalities to ensure the final version of the video flowed and felt easy to understand. My goal was to create a fast-paced, interactive "game show" experience that felt professional and fun at the same time, and I think I was able to achieve that.

I Found Geometry Dash's STRANGEST Players...

(9:26 – 11:25) This documentary-style project explores the history and rise of two of Geometry Dash’s most influential level creators, Zobros and KrmaL. My work on this video focused on a chapter covering the rise of KrmaL, where I highlighted his first of many levels that helped but his name on the map in the Geometry Dash community. The goal of this video was to bring an investigative tone while sshowcasing the "strange" and unconventional level design choices that defined this era of the game’s history.

This was my first professional collaboration working as a video editor for another YouTuber, MatMart. It was a unique experience that brought its own set of challenges. It required me to step outside of my own personal style and seamlessly match the established pacing and visual language of another creator's channel. Not only did this help me broaden my knowledge of video editing techniques and styles, but it also helped build my portfolio for other potential YouTubers in need of editors to see.

Can You Beat Geometry Dash With a Wii Remote?

In this experimental challenge video, I set out to become the first person to complete an "Extreme Demon" — the highest tier of difficulty in Geometry Dash — using a Wii Remote. While the community had seen other figures play Geometry Dash with a Wii Remote and other obscure controllers in the past, none had successfully conquered a level of this caliber. The project follows my progression from basic levels to the ultimate goal of beating "Acu," documenting the hurdles of using a motion controller from 2006 to play a game that requires frame-perfect precision.

Again, I had never made a video of this genre before; because much of the process involved repetitive "grinding," I had to rely on creative editing to keep the momentum high. I utilized nostalgic Wii-themed assets and music to lean into the nostalgia and "magic" of the console, while maintaining a high-stakes, documentary-style narrative during the final attempts. Completing this challenge in under 1,000 attempts and three hours of playtime was a surprise to me, and the final result remains one of the most unique milestones in my creative portfolio.

The Most Emotional Album of All Time

This is a video essay that explores Everywhere at the End of Time, a 6-part music album by The Caretaker that sonically depicts the stages of dementia and Alzheimer's disease. The video uses various film theory techniques to analyze _____. I focused on the use of distortion and metadiagetic sound — audio that exists within a character's mind — to draw comparisons to the techniques used in films like Black Swan and Three Colors: Blue.

This was created as a final project for my Analysis Moving Image class at Quinnipiac University in 2024. I spent 39 consecutive hours completing this video, handling every part of the creative process by myself (scriptwriting, sound design, research, voice-overs, and editing). Beyond the fact that it was created for an assignment, this video was deeply personal to me, as my grandmother was diagnosed with Alzheimer's during the COVID-19 pandemic. I tried to make the viewers feel the weight and pain of dementia while learning something in the process, but I also wanted to bring a unique perspective on the disease, in which there could be a "strange beauty" in memories fading away.

"The Board" // 1-Line Short Film

This short film follows a detective reviewing an investigation board filled with suspects, evidence, and connecting threads. One by one, he crosses off each individual until only a single person remains. After circling the final target, he delivers the line, “If karma is real, it hasn’t gotten me yet.” As he exits the frame, hidden writing on the back of the board, reading "The Killing Board," is revealed, shifting the narrative and implying that he is not a detective, but a killer choosing his next victim.

This project was created in under 24 hours for my Intermediate Production course at Quinnipiac University, where the challenge was to build a story around a single randomized line of dialogue. I handled all aspects of production myself, including cinematography, lighting, sound design, and editing, which forced me to think more deeply about how to build tension and deliver a strong, unexpected reveal when there is very little information to go on.
